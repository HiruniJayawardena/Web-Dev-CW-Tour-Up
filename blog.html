Kandy

A lot of travelers visit Sri Lanka for its white-sand beaches and world-class surfing, but the island's mountainous, pearl-shrouded center also contains a spiritually appealing zone with tea farms, misty hills, and those iconic blue train carriages.

Kandy, also known as the City of Kings, is Sri Lanka's second city and the undisputed historical and cultural center of the island. It serves as the entrance to all of this hill country action (as well as the best train trip in the world!).

The strong ancient Kingdom of Kandy, shielded by its location and less-accessible terrain, was able to fend off both Portuguese and Dutch colonizers during the 1500s and 1600s, becoming the last stronghold of independent Ceylon. Up until 1815, when it ultimately submitted to British rule, the city was able to maintain the distinctive customs, culture, and arts that had been severely suppressed elsewhere in the nation.

The numerous important cultural and historic locations in the city still preserve that culture today, serving as the inspiration for all the top attractions in Kandy. The most well-known of these is the Temple of the Tooth relic, which is also the most significant sacred site in Buddhism and is thought to contain a piece of Buddha's tooth.

Kandy is a city of delightful chaos with bustling streets, historic temples, colonial architecture, aristocratic gardens, and the magnificent Kandy Lake as the background to a perfect few days in Sri Lanka's verdant hills, but it is also much more than just its main draw.

Galle

The stunning UNESCO World Heritage Site of Galle is located on Sri Lanka's southern shore. There are many fascinating places to explore in this old town, including its charming and winding lanes, commanding Dutch colonial structures, historic mosques, churches, and the well-known lighthouse. The substantial ramparts that the Dutch merchants who ruled the city in the 17th century constructed can be traveled by walking.

Galle was initially taken over by the Portuguese in the late 16th century, but the Dutch seized it very rapidly in the following century. After that, for more than 200 years, it developed into a very significant spice trading center.

Within the many historical structures that make up the fort's surroundings, the 300-year-old Dutch atmosphere is still very much alive. The community has expanded significantly over the years and now encompasses a large area. The beating heart of Galle's past, however, is and will always be the Dutch Fort. Since its construction in the early 16th century, the fortified city has maintained its historical character. The city of Galle is a cosmopolitan mix of cultures, religions, and fascinating Dutch imperial structures.

Additionally, there are lots of adorable boutique stores, cozy cafés, and restaurants where you can refuel.

Dambulla

Dambulla is situated in Sri Lanka's Central Province in the Matale District, 148 kilometers (92 miles) to the northeast of Colombo and 72 kilometers (45 miles) to the north of Kandy. It is the largest center for vegetable distribution in the Sri Lanka because of its position at a significant intersection.

The biggest and best preserved cave temple complex in Sri Lanka and the Rangiri Dambulla International Stadium, known for being constructed in just 167 days, are two of the area's main draws. Additionally, the region is home to the Iron Wood Forest, also known as Na Uyana Aranya, and South Asia's biggest range of rose quartz mountains.

The most recent archaeological site of significant historical importance discovered in Dambulla is the Ibbankatuwa prehistoric burial site, which is close to the cave temple complexes. It is only 3 km (1.9 mi) away from the cave temples and provides proof of the existence of indigenous civilisations long before Indian influence arrived on the Island nation.

As above mentioned here are the places that you can visit around Dambulla,

  • Rangiri Dambulla International Stadium
  • Na Uyana Aranya

Sigiriya

One of the most well-known sights to see in Sri Lanka is Lion Rock in Sigiriya, a huge (200 m) square-shaped rock that was once King Kassapa's fortress and has since become home to murals, gardens, and, on top of the rock, a swimming pool and palace. Lion Rock is one of the eight UNESCO World Heritage sites and is a must-see in Sri Lanka!

Get ready for a little challenge and climb the more than 1200 stairs to the highest point of Lion Rock. Do not be afraid; Good paths and steps are used to mark out the climb. The most impressive view of the mountain is provided by beginning at the terrace gardens.

You can take a break at the the mirror wall, and the Sigiriya Frescoes (old wall paintings).Enjoy the breathtaking 360-degree view of the entire area once you reach the highest point of the rock. Every angle offers a different perspective.

To avoid the heat and crowds of people, climbing Lion Rock is advised starting at 7 AM. Approximately 45 minutes will be needed to reach the reaching the top. You will need two to three hours to thoroughly investigate the entire region beneath and above the rock.

Udawalawa

One of the best spots to go on a safari in Sri Lanka is Udawalawe National Park! There, you will likely find a lot of creatures, including elephants. One of the best activities to do in Sri Lanka is to visit Udawalawe!

With 30.8 hectares, Udawalawe Park is one of Sri Lanka's biggest parks. In 1972, the Sri Lankan government established the Udawalawe National Park to safeguard the wildlife. There are thought to be about 500 elephants living on the 30,000 hectares. There are additional animals that you can see. Indeed, there are almost 250 different bird types and about 40 different mammal species.

Early in the morning or late in the day are the two ideal times of day to go on a wildlife drive in the park.Tours in the afternoon are frequently less crowded, and you can see the stunning dusk inside the park.

And the safari usually lasts for 2 to 3 hours.We can guarantee that you can really enjoy it and see a lot of wildlife.

Hikkaduwa

Hikkaduwa is a popular beach town located on the southern coast of Sri Lanka, known for its pristine beaches, crystal-clear waters, and rich marine life. The town is a famous tourist destination and offers a wide range of activities such as surfing, snorkeling, scuba diving, and swimming

Hikkaduwa has a vibrant nightlife, with many restaurants, bars, and clubs offering local and international cuisine and entertainment. The town also has many shops and markets selling souvenirs, handicrafts, and clothing.

Tourists can explore the nearby Hikkaduwa National Park, which is home to a variety of wildlife, including turtles, crocodiles, and birds. The park also features a coral sanctuary, where visitors can see colorful coral reefs and exotic fish species

Overall, Hikkaduwa is an excellent destination for tourists looking to enjoy sun, sea, and sand, as well as explore Sri Lanka's natural beauty and cultural heritage.

Ella

Ella is one of the most enchanting locations on Sri Lanka. It is hidden away in the misty high country of Sri Lanka, among idyllic green mountains and gushing waterfalls. Many of favorite Sri Lankan adventures can be found right here, including scaling the highest point of Ella Rock, seeing the enigmatic Nine Arch Bridge , standing on the brink of the thundering Diyaluma Falls, the second-tallest in the Sri Lanka, and riding the fabled Kandy to Ella train through tea plantations and mountain passes.

Ella is a pleasant and laid-back town ideal for relaxing, hiking, and escaping sweltering southern coast temperatures. It is a location where Sri Lanka's colonial past coexists with its thriving Sinhalese and Tamil cultures, and where a wealth of natural encounters can be found alongside a wealth of cultural ones. And it's a location where mastering the art of cooking delectable Sri Lankan food is a necessity.

Kitulgala

Kitulgala, a small town on the Kelani River in the Western hills and the center of all of Sri Lanka's adventure sports, gives tourists seeking an action-packed vacation the thrills of white-water rafting, jungle trekking, bird watching, and cave exploration.

Additionally, it is a well-liked location for training programs. The area, which is located in the wet zone and experiences two monsoons annually, is home to rolling hills, tropical rain forests, and winding waterways.

The river provides both seasoned and inexperienced paddlers with enough thrills for whitewater enthusiasts. You must, however, do so through a reputable, licensed, and experienced operator with knowledgeable aides. Rafting is available in Kitulgala on Grade 3 and Grade 4 rapids that plunge through jungle, tea, and rubber farms as they tumble down the Kelani River. A tiny primitive forest off the river that is home to about 54 rare bird species provides thrilling explorations for nature lovers.
